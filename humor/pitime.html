It's about "π" time!

     Most people in school math classes are aware of a special event day, called Pi Day. It's usually observed on or about March 14 of the year. This is because the usual way to write that date is 3/14, and the usual approximation of π is 3.14. To learn about some nice ways to observe the "pi" moment, read the article "Let's Take Another Look At Pi Day" found in this website.

     This page takes up the matter once again, but with an interesting twist, we think.

     First, we must introduce another math concept, one not so familiar to many people: the greatest integer function. Simply explained, given any number that has a "decimal" part, like 5.26 for example, the greatest integer of 5.26 is 5, the part to the left of the decimal point.

     Mathematicians have a special notation to express this. Using our example above, they would do it like this:

[ 5.26 ] = 5
.

     So now bringing in π again, we could say:

[ π ] = 3

     But we don't intend to stop there. Watch what happens if we insert the number 100 in the following way:

[ 100π ] = 314

     This is certainly true because as π begins 3.141592, then 100 times that number would be 314.1592 And the rest is obvious.

     This new number of 314 can lead us to some new explorations. My first thought is this:

What is the 314th day of a year?

     Since a year has 365 days (366 in leap years), there must be a day that is #314, no?

     Now let's not stop here either.

What is the 314th hour of the year?

     Obviously it must occur in January, but on what day and at what specific hour? Return to the month of March, when Pi Day is normally celebrated.

What is the 314th hour of that month?

We're on a roll now!

Additional ideas that spring to mind could include:

  1. What is the 314th minute, or second, of a given day? (Give time in hh:mm:ss form.)
  2. When will the 314th week, or month, occur in a given decade? (State on what day of which month that week would start.)
  3. When were you, dear reader, 314 days (or weeks or months) old?

     No one said we had to use 100 as our power of 10 above, right? So, what would happen if we investigated the possibilities of these expressions?

[ 1000π ]

or

[ 10000π ]

     Perhaps you can begin to see the possibilities for your own creativity, right?

Postscript:

     The greatest integer function is sometimes called the floor function. Additional technical information about this concept can be found here (http://mathworld.wolfram.com/FloorFunction.html ) and many other sites in the web.
